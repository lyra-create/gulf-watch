Family Safety Guide

What families in the UAE need to know right now.

What to do during an alert

During a siren or explosion — go inside immediately. The instinct to step outside and look at the sky is natural — and it's dangerous. Interceptions happen overhead, and debris falls in unpredictable patterns.

Go inside now

Move to an interior room on a lower floor. Stay away from windows. Flying glass is one of the main causes of shrapnel injuries.

Interior room, lower floor

Bathrooms and hallways are good options — no exterior walls, no windows. Lie flat or crouch under a sturdy table if you can.

Windows are the danger

Even intercepted missiles cause pressure waves and fragments. Shattered glass can travel 15–20 metres. Keep curtains closed as a basic precaution.

Don't go outside to look

Interception debris falls silently. There's no warning when a fragment begins to fall. Looking at the light show from outside puts you directly in the risk zone.

If you're outside when it happens

Find solid cover fast

Move to a building entrance, underpass, or concrete structure. Avoid open ground, glass facades, and parked vehicles near buildings.

If no cover is available

Lie flat on the ground, face down. Cover your head and neck with your arms. Debris and shrapnel travel horizontally as well as downward.

After the alert

Leave all debris alone

Objects on the ground may include unexploded ordnance, fuel residue, or hazardous materials. Report locations to authorities via 999 (UAE Emergency).

Notify your family

After the all-clear, contact family members to confirm everyone is safe. Keep communication brief — phone networks can be congested after incidents.

A 72-hour kit matters. It doesn't need to be military-grade. It needs to be ready. Keep it in one bag, in one place. Every adult in the household should know where it is.

Water & Food
💧
Water — 3 litres per person per day. Keep a minimum 72-hour supply — that's 9 litres per person. Don't rely on tap water in an emergency.
🥫
Food — Non-perishable food for 3 days. Canned goods, nuts, dried fruit, crackers, protein bars. Think calorie-dense items that need no cooking.
🍼
Baby/child supplies — If you have young children: formula, pureed food, snacks they'll actually eat. Children often refuse unfamiliar food when stressed.
Documents & Money
📄
Passports and IDs — Store originals in a waterproof zip-lock bag inside your kit. Include insurance cards, residence permits, medical cards.
💵
Cash — AED 500–2000 in small notes. ATMs go offline in emergencies. Cards sometimes don't work. Cash is always liquid.
💊
Medication — A full 7-day supply of any regular medications, including children's medicines. Keep prescriptions with them.
Communication & Power
🔋
Power bank — At least 20,000mAh. Keep it charged. Phone battery is your lifeline.
📻
Battery-powered radio — For UAE emergency broadcasts if internet goes down. Frequencies: Dubai 92.0 FM, Abu Dhabi 99.3 FM.
🔦
Torch + batteries — A basic head torch is more useful than a handheld one if you're moving with children.
Health & Safety
🩹
First aid kit — Bandages, antiseptic wipes, pain relief (adult + children's), plasters, a tourniquet. Replace anything you use.
😷
N95 masks — Debris, dust, and smoke from structural fires. One per person. Keep sealed.
📢
Whistle — To signal rescuers if you're trapped or separated in darkness. Attach one to children's bags or clothing.
🔌
Spare chargers and cables — One cable per device type. USB-C, Lightning, whatever your family uses.
For children specifically
🧸
Familiar comfort item — A toy, stuffed animal, or blanket from home. Children regulate fear better when they have something familiar. Don't sacrifice this for space — it matters.
🎨
Activity bag — Books, small toys, colouring materials. Waiting is hard for young children. Boredom during a shelter-in-place can become fear very quickly.

The way you respond is what they remember.

Children take their emotional cues from the adults around them. Your voice, your posture, your words — they read all of it. The goal isn't to hide what's happening. It's to help them feel safe and capable inside it.

Talking to young children (3–6 years)
What to say

Use straightforward language. You don't need to explain geopolitics. "Sometimes there are big bangs in the sky. When that happens, we go inside so we're safe." That's enough.

When they're scared

"It sounds scary. That makes sense. I feel worried sometimes too." Acknowledging fear is the first step to calming it. Dismissing it ("there's nothing to be scared of") doesn't work — they already feel it.

"It's okay to feel scared. I'm here with you, and we're safe right now."

Make them feel capable

Children feel less helpless when they have a job. "Your job is to hold this torch." "Can you be the one to carry the bag?" Small responsibilities help.

"Can you be my helper? I need you to carry this — it's important."

Normal is safe

Keep bedtime, meals, and stories the same. Disrupted routines signal danger to young children even more than words do. If you can keep the bedtime story, keep it.

What they overhear matters

Turn off news when children are present. Adult conversations about the situation should happen out of earshot. They don't need all the detail — they need to feel held.

For children aged 7–12
They'll ask directly

Older children will ask "are we going to be okay?" Be honest without catastrophising. "I don't know exactly what's going to happen. What I know is we're doing everything we can to be safe."

Emphasise protection

The army, the police, the civil defence — people are working to keep us safe. Children feel better knowing there are competent adults in charge beyond their immediate family.

"There are people whose whole job is to protect us. They're working right now."

Signs to look for

Sleep problems, clinginess, aggression, loss of appetite — these are normal stress responses in children, not permanent damage. If they persist for more than 2 weeks, speak to a professional.

What not to say
  • "Don't worry, everything's fine." — They know something is wrong. It erodes trust.
  • "Stop being scared, you're a big kid." — Feelings aren't a weakness.
  • "I don't know" without following up. — Uncertainty is okay. Abandonment isn't.
  • Explaining the geopolitical context to a 5-year-old. — They can't process it. It just adds fear.
